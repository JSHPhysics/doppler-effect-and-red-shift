Classical Doppler — a source moves through space; wavelengths shift on emission.

How to use this simulation
  1. Move the Source velocity slider. Positive = source moves toward you (blueshift); negative = source moves away (redshift).
  2. Watch the wavefronts compress in front and spread out behind. The spectrum strip below shifts in step.
  3. The source pulses green at every emission (a constant rate). The observer pulses blue or red at every arrival — its rate differs from the emission rate, and that difference is the Doppler shift you can see directly.
  4. If the source travels past the edge of the canvas it keeps moving and keeps emitting — an arrow on the edge marks where it has gone. Watch what happens to the observer's flash colour as the source passes the observer (blueshift switches to redshift). Press Reset when you want to recentre the source.
  5. Press Pause when you want a stable picture to take a measurement.
  6. Use the Measure & calculate panel below to apply the formula yourself, then check.

The two kinds of redshift

Doppler shift

A Doppler shift is caused by a source moving through space relative to the observer. The wavelength change happens at emission: if the source moves toward you each successive wavefront is launched from a slightly closer position, so the spacing you measure is smaller. For speeds well below light speed the textbook formula is z ≈ v/c (negative z = blueshift, source approaching). The classic example is a nearby star moving in our galaxy, or an ambulance siren.

Cosmological redshift

For very distant galaxies the photon's wavelength stretches during its journey because space itself expands while the photon travels. Galaxies aren't really moving through space — their comoving coordinates are fixed; the metric of space scales up. The relation is 1 + z = aobs/aemit. This is the redshift seen for distant galaxies and is the basis of Hubble's law and the evidence for an expanding universe.

For nearby galaxies the two formulas give the same answer — that's why z = v/c is taught first. For distant galaxies, only the cosmological formula is correct.
